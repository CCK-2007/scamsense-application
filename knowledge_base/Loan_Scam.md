# Loan Scam

## Overview

A loan scam is a type of fraud in which scammers target individuals seeking personal, business or emergency loans by offering fake loan services or impersonating legitimate financial institutions and licensed moneylenders. These scams typically promise fast approval, low interest rates or guaranteed loans regardless of the applicant's financial history. Once victims express interest, scammers request advance payments, processing fees, insurance charges or personal information before the loan is supposedly disbursed. In reality, no legitimate loan is provided, and victims often lose money or have their personal information misused. Loan scams commonly exploit individuals experiencing financial difficulties or those urgently requiring financial assistance.

## Common Scam Tactics

- Advertising guaranteed loan approval regardless of credit history or income.
- Promising unusually low interest rates or highly favourable repayment terms.
- Claiming that loans can be approved immediately with minimal documentation.
- Requesting upfront payments for processing, administrative, insurance or legal fees.
- Impersonating banks, licensed moneylenders or financial service providers.
- Using fake websites, mobile applications or social media advertisements to appear legitimate.
- Sending fraudulent loan approval letters or contracts.
- Requesting sensitive personal or banking information during the application process.
- Claiming additional payments are required before the loan can be released.
- Continuing to request new fees after earlier payments have already been made.

## Common Warning Signs

- Loan approval is guaranteed without assessing financial eligibility.
- Requests for advance payments before loan disbursement.
- Pressure to make immediate payments to secure the loan.
- Communication primarily conducted through personal messaging applications.
- Unlicensed lenders or companies with limited verifiable information.
- Websites or emails containing spelling mistakes or unofficial domain names.
- Requests for passwords, One-Time Passwords (OTPs) or online banking credentials.
- Loan offers received without submitting an application.
- Frequent changes to payment requirements during the application process.
- Representatives refusing to provide official company or licensing information.

## Typical Victim Requests

- Pay processing, administration or insurance fees before receiving the loan.
- Transfer money to verify identity or demonstrate financial capability.
- Submit NRIC details, passport information or other personal identification documents.
- Provide bank account information and supporting financial documents.
- Share online banking credentials or authentication codes.
- Sign loan agreements before independently verifying the lender.
- Download unfamiliar applications for loan processing.
- Make additional payments after being told the initial fees were insufficient.

## Prevention Tips

- Verify that the lender is legitimate before applying for a loan.
- Be cautious of loan offers promising guaranteed approval or unusually attractive terms.
- Never pay upfront fees before receiving a legitimate loan.
- Confirm company information using official websites and published contact details.
- Compare loan offers from multiple reputable financial institutions.
- Read loan agreements carefully before signing any documents.
- Never disclose passwords, One-Time Passwords (OTPs) or online banking credentials.
- Take time to research unfamiliar lenders before providing personal information.
- Seek financial advice if uncertain about a loan offer.

## Recommended Actions

- Stop communicating with the suspected scammer immediately.
- Do not make additional payments or provide further personal information.
- Contact your bank immediately if financial information has been disclosed or money has been transferred.
- Preserve loan advertisements, contracts, payment records and conversations as evidence.
- Verify the lender's legitimacy before taking further action.
- Report the suspected scam to the Singapore Police Force (SPF).
- Monitor bank accounts and credit records for suspicious activity.
- Change passwords and authentication methods if account credentials may have been compromised.

## Singapore Context

Loan scams are regularly reported in Singapore and often involve scammers impersonating licensed moneylenders, banks or financial institutions. Victims commonly encounter fake loan advertisements through SMS messages, social media platforms, messaging applications and online advertisements. Scammers frequently target individuals experiencing financial difficulties by promising quick approval and requiring advance payments before the loan is supposedly released.

The Singapore Police Force (SPF) regularly advises members of the public that legitimate financial institutions and licensed moneylenders do not require borrowers to pay processing fees before loan disbursement. The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) encourages consumers to obtain financial services only from authorised institutions and to verify unfamiliar financial offers carefully. ScamShield helps reduce exposure to scam messages and calls, while the Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) promotes cybersecurity awareness to reduce phishing and online financial fraud.

## Additional Notes

Loan scams primarily exploit financial stress, urgency and the desire for immediate financial assistance. Victims may believe that advance payments are necessary administrative procedures, especially when scammers provide convincing documents, professional websites or persuasive explanations. However, repeated requests for additional fees after initial payments are a common characteristic of fraudulent loan schemes.

Legitimate lenders generally evaluate applicants through established financial assessment procedures and clearly explain loan terms, interest rates and repayment obligations. Users should independently verify the identity of any lender, carefully review all documentation and avoid making financial decisions based solely on unsolicited loan offers or promises of guaranteed approval. If a lender requests payment before releasing a loan, the offer should be treated with extreme caution.
