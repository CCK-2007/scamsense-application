# Lottery Scam

## Overview

A lottery scam is a type of fraud in which scammers falsely inform individuals that they have won a lottery, lucky draw, sweepstake or prize despite never participating in such a competition. The scam is designed to convince victims that they are entitled to a valuable reward but must first complete certain requirements before receiving their winnings. These requirements often involve paying fees, providing personal information or sharing banking details. Lottery scams commonly arrive through SMS messages, emails, phone calls, social media platforms or messaging applications and often use the names of well-known companies or international organisations to appear credible.

## Common Scam Tactics

- Claiming that the victim has won a lottery, lucky draw or sweepstake without entering any competition.
- Impersonating well-known companies, international organisations or promotional campaigns.
- Requesting payment of taxes, administrative charges, processing fees or delivery costs before releasing the prize.
- Sending fake certificates, award letters or winning notifications to increase credibility.
- Claiming that the prize must be collected within a limited period to create urgency.
- Requesting personal information for so-called prize verification.
- Directing victims to communicate through unofficial email addresses or messaging applications.
- Using fake websites that imitate legitimate lottery organisations.
- Promising luxury prizes such as cash rewards, vehicles, holidays or expensive electronic devices.
- Continuing to request additional payments after the victim has already paid earlier fees.

## Common Warning Signs

- Receiving notifications about prizes without entering any competition.
- Requests for advance payments before the prize can be claimed.
- Claims that taxes or administrative fees must be paid upfront.
- Messages containing unrealistic prize amounts or extravagant rewards.
- Pressure to respond immediately to avoid losing the prize.
- Requests for confidential personal or banking information.
- Communication from unofficial email domains or unfamiliar phone numbers.
- Poor grammar, spelling mistakes or inconsistent formatting.
- Instructions to keep the prize confidential.
- Requests to transfer money through unusual payment methods.

## Typical Victim Requests

- Pay processing, administrative or tax fees before collecting the prize.
- Provide NRIC details, passport information or other personal identification documents.
- Share bank account details for prize transfers.
- Submit copies of identification documents for verification.
- Click links leading to fake prize claim websites.
- Contact designated representatives through unofficial communication channels.
- Purchase gift cards or transfer money to release the winnings.
- Make repeated payments for additional verification or delivery charges.

## Prevention Tips

- Remember that legitimate lotteries require participants to enter before prizes can be awarded.
- Be sceptical of unexpected prize notifications, especially if no competition was entered.
- Never pay fees to claim lottery winnings or promotional prizes.
- Verify promotional campaigns through official company websites.
- Avoid clicking links contained in unsolicited prize notifications.
- Protect personal and financial information from unknown individuals.
- Research unfamiliar organisations before responding.
- Discuss suspicious prize notifications with trusted family members or friends.
- Stay informed about common lottery scam techniques.

## Recommended Actions

- Ignore and delete suspicious prize notifications.
- Do not make payments or provide personal information.
- Do not click links or download attachments from unsolicited messages.
- Report suspicious messages through ScamShield where applicable.
- Contact your bank immediately if financial information has been disclosed or payments have been made.
- Preserve emails, SMS messages and payment records as evidence.
- Report the scam to the Singapore Police Force (SPF) if financial loss has occurred.
- Monitor financial accounts for suspicious activity.

## Singapore Context

Lottery scams continue to affect individuals in Singapore through SMS messages, emails, messaging applications and social media platforms. Victims are commonly informed that they have won international lotteries, overseas sweepstakes or promotional campaigns organised by well-known brands despite having no prior participation. The scammers then request administrative fees, taxes or processing charges before the prize can supposedly be released.

The Singapore Police Force (SPF) regularly publishes advisories reminding the public that legitimate prize organisers do not require winners to pay upfront fees before receiving their prizes. ScamShield helps users reduce exposure to scam messages and calls, while the Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) promotes cybersecurity awareness to help users recognise phishing attempts and fraudulent online promotions. Members of the public are encouraged to verify promotional campaigns through official company websites rather than relying on information provided in unsolicited messages.

## Additional Notes

Lottery scams rely heavily on excitement, curiosity and the desire for unexpected financial gain. Scammers often create convincing prize notifications using official-looking logos, certificates and congratulatory messages to make victims believe that the rewards are genuine. However, legitimate lottery organisers and promotional campaigns generally follow transparent procedures and do not require advance payments before distributing prizes.

Users should remember a simple principle: if they did not enter a lottery, lucky draw or sweepstake, they cannot legitimately win one. Even if the notification appears professional or references a well-known company, any request for upfront payment, confidential information or urgent action should be treated as a significant warning sign. Verifying promotional claims independently through official sources is one of the most effective ways to avoid becoming a victim of lottery scams.
