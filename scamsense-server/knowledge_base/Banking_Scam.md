# Banking Scam

## Overview

A banking scam is a type of fraud in which criminals impersonate banks, financial institutions, payment service providers or trusted organisations to deceive individuals into revealing sensitive financial information or authorising fraudulent transactions. These scams commonly occur through SMS messages, phone calls, emails, messaging applications and fake websites that closely resemble legitimate banking platforms. Scammers often create a false sense of urgency by claiming that a bank account has been compromised, unusual transactions have been detected or immediate verification is required. Their ultimate objective is to gain access to bank accounts, steal money or obtain confidential information such as passwords, One-Time Passwords (OTPs), PINs and online banking credentials.

## Common Scam Tactics

- Impersonating banks through SMS, emails or phone calls using official-looking names and branding.
- Sending phishing links that direct victims to fake online banking login pages.
- Claiming that suspicious transactions or security breaches require immediate verification.
- Requesting victims to confirm banking credentials, passwords or security questions.
- Asking victims to provide One-Time Passwords (OTPs) under the pretext of identity verification.
- Convincing victims to install remote access applications that allow scammers to control their devices.
- Spoofing caller identification so incoming calls appear to originate from legitimate banks.
- Pretending to be bank officers conducting fraud investigations or account recovery procedures.
- Encouraging victims to transfer funds to a so-called "safe" or "secure" account controlled by scammers.
- Using fear, urgency or authority to pressure victims into acting without verifying the request.

## Common Warning Signs

- Unexpected messages claiming that a bank account has been suspended or compromised.
- Requests for confidential banking information through SMS, email or phone calls.
- Messages containing shortened or unfamiliar website links.
- Poor grammar, unusual wording or formatting inconsistencies in communications.
- Urgent deadlines that discourage careful verification.
- Requests to download unfamiliar applications or software.
- Phone calls demanding immediate action to prevent financial loss.
- Emails or websites with domain names that differ slightly from official bank websites.
- Unexpected requests for OTPs, PINs or security codes.
- Pressure to bypass normal banking procedures.

## Typical Victim Requests

- Log in to a banking account using a provided link.
- Reveal online banking usernames and passwords.
- Share One-Time Passwords (OTPs) or authentication codes.
- Confirm debit or credit card information.
- Transfer money to another bank account for "security purposes."
- Install remote access or screen-sharing software.
- Verify personal identity by submitting NRIC numbers or other personal information.
- Approve banking transactions that were initiated by scammers.
- Disable security features such as transaction alerts or authentication measures.

## Prevention Tips

- Access online banking services only through official banking applications or websites.
- Never share passwords, PINs or OTPs with anyone, regardless of who they claim to be.
- Verify suspicious messages by contacting the bank through official customer service channels.
- Carefully inspect website addresses before entering login credentials.
- Enable multi-factor authentication and banking transaction notifications whenever available.
- Keep mobile devices and banking applications updated with the latest security patches.
- Avoid clicking links received through unsolicited SMS messages or emails.
- Be cautious of callers requesting immediate financial actions.
- Regularly review bank account activity for unauthorised transactions.

## Recommended Actions

- Stop communicating with the suspected scammer immediately.
- Do not click additional links or provide further personal information.
- Contact the bank using its official hotline to verify the message or call.
- Freeze or temporarily suspend affected bank cards or accounts if credentials may have been compromised.
- Change online banking passwords and update authentication methods.
- Report suspicious communications through ScamShield where applicable.
- File a police report if financial losses or attempted fraud have occurred.
- Monitor bank accounts closely for any unauthorised transactions.
- Preserve screenshots, messages, emails and transaction records as evidence.

## Singapore Context

Banking scams are among the most frequently reported scam categories in Singapore and often involve SMS phishing, fake banking websites and impersonation of financial institutions. Victims are commonly deceived into revealing banking credentials or authorising fraudulent transfers through convincing social engineering techniques.

Individuals are encouraged to verify suspicious banking communications directly with their financial institution instead of responding to unsolicited messages. The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) works with financial institutions to strengthen banking security measures, while the Singapore Police Force (SPF) regularly publishes scam advisories and emerging scam trends. ScamShield provides tools that help users identify and reduce exposure to scam calls and messages. The Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) also promotes cybersecurity awareness and safe online practices to reduce phishing and credential theft.

## Additional Notes

Banking scams primarily rely on social engineering rather than technical hacking. Scammers exploit fear, urgency and trust to persuade victims into voluntarily disclosing sensitive information or approving transactions. Modern banking scams often imitate genuine communications with remarkable accuracy, making them difficult to distinguish from legitimate notifications based solely on appearance.

A genuine bank will generally not request passwords, PINs or One-Time Passwords through phone calls, SMS messages or emails. Users should independently verify unexpected requests using official contact information rather than details provided in suspicious communications. Even when a message appears authentic, verifying its legitimacy before taking action significantly reduces the risk of financial loss.
