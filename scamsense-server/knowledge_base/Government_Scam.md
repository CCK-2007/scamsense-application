# Government Scam

## Overview

A government scam is a type of fraud in which scammers impersonate government agencies, public officials or regulatory authorities to deceive individuals into providing money, personal information or access to sensitive accounts. These scams commonly involve fake phone calls, SMS messages, emails or letters claiming that the victim has violated a law, owes outstanding fines, has unclaimed government benefits or is involved in a criminal investigation. Scammers rely on the authority and credibility associated with government institutions to create fear and urgency, pressuring victims into complying without independently verifying the claims.

## Common Scam Tactics

- Impersonating government agencies through phone calls, SMS messages or emails.
- Claiming that the victim has unpaid taxes, fines or legal penalties.
- Pretending that the victim is under criminal investigation for offences such as money laundering or identity theft.
- Threatening arrest, legal action or account suspension unless immediate action is taken.
- Sending phishing links to fake government websites that request personal information.
- Requesting payment of administrative fees, penalties or processing charges.
- Asking victims to verify their identity by providing personal or financial information.
- Spoofing official phone numbers, email addresses or government logos to appear legitimate.
- Claiming that government grants, subsidies or financial assistance require advance payments or verification.
- Transferring victims between multiple fake officials to create the appearance of an ongoing investigation.

## Common Warning Signs

- Unexpected contact from a government agency demanding immediate action.
- Threats of arrest, prosecution or legal consequences if payment is not made immediately.
- Requests for payment through bank transfers, gift cards or cryptocurrency.
- Messages containing suspicious links to unofficial government websites.
- Requests for passwords, One-Time Passwords (OTPs) or banking credentials.
- High-pressure tactics discouraging victims from seeking advice or verification.
- Instructions to keep the investigation confidential.
- Poor grammar, unusual wording or inconsistent formatting in official-looking communications.
- Contact from unofficial email addresses or personal phone numbers.
- Requests for payment before official documentation is provided.

## Typical Victim Requests

- Pay outstanding fines or administrative fees immediately.
- Transfer money to designated bank accounts for investigation or verification purposes.
- Provide NRIC numbers, passport details or other personal identification documents.
- Share banking information or authentication codes.
- Click links to verify identity or update personal records.
- Download software or applications for identity verification.
- Remain on the phone throughout the investigation process.
- Avoid informing family members, banks or other authorities about the ongoing investigation.

## Prevention Tips

- Verify unexpected communications by contacting the relevant government agency through its official website or hotline.
- Do not rely on phone numbers or links provided in unsolicited messages.
- Never share passwords, One-Time Passwords (OTPs) or banking credentials with anyone.
- Be cautious of threats demanding immediate payment or legal action.
- Avoid clicking links received through unsolicited SMS messages or emails.
- Check whether the communication originates from an official government domain.
- Take time to verify claims before responding or making payments.
- Discuss suspicious communications with trusted family members or friends if uncertain.
- Stay informed about the latest scam advisories issued by government agencies.

## Recommended Actions

- End communication with the suspected scammer immediately.
- Do not make payments or disclose additional personal information.
- Verify the claims by contacting the relevant government agency using official contact information.
- Report suspicious calls or messages through ScamShield where appropriate.
- Contact your bank immediately if financial information has been disclosed or money has been transferred.
- Preserve screenshots, emails, SMS messages and call records as evidence.
- File a police report if financial loss has occurred or sensitive information has been compromised.
- Monitor financial accounts and personal records for suspicious activity.

## Singapore Context

Government impersonation scams are regularly reported in Singapore and often involve scammers pretending to represent agencies such as the Singapore Police Force (SPF), the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS), the Immigration & Checkpoints Authority (ICA), the Ministry of Manpower (MOM), or other public institutions. Victims may receive calls claiming they are involved in criminal investigations, have immigration issues, owe taxes or are required to verify personal information.

The Singapore Police Force (SPF) frequently publishes advisories warning the public about government impersonation scams and reminds individuals that legitimate government agencies do not demand immediate payments through unofficial methods or request sensitive banking credentials over the phone. ScamShield helps reduce exposure to scam calls and messages, while the Cyber Security Agency of Singapore (CSA) promotes cybersecurity awareness and safe online practices. The Ministry of Manpower (MOM) also issues public advisories regarding scams targeting job seekers and work pass holders, while the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) may issue warnings when scams involve financial institutions or banking services.

## Additional Notes

Government scams are particularly effective because they exploit authority, fear and uncertainty. Scammers often attempt to convince victims that failing to comply immediately will result in severe legal or financial consequences. In reality, legitimate government agencies generally follow established procedures and provide official documentation when necessary. They do not request passwords, One-Time Passwords (OTPs) or immediate transfers to personal bank accounts during investigations.

Users should remember that caller identification, official logos and professional language can all be forged. Regardless of how convincing a communication appears, independent verification through official government websites or published contact numbers is one of the most effective ways to prevent becoming a victim of government impersonation scams.
